# Question Design Principles

## Core Principles for Effective Assessment Questions

### 1. Clarity and Precision
- **Clear Language**: Use simple, direct language appropriate for student level
- **Unambiguous Instructions**: Avoid multiple interpretations
- **Specific Requirements**: Clearly state what is expected
- **Appropriate Vocabulary**: Match terminology to student knowledge level

**Example of Poor Clarity**: "Discuss the thing about photosynthesis"
**Example of Good Clarity**: "Explain the role of chlorophyll in the light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis"

### 2. Alignment with Learning Objectives
- Questions should directly assess stated learning outcomes
- Match cognitive level of question to intended objective
- Ensure content relevance to course material
- Test important concepts, not trivial details

### 3. Appropriate Difficulty Level
- **Easy Questions (70-90% success)**: Basic recall, fundamental concepts
- **Medium Questions (40-70% success)**: Application, analysis of familiar content
- **Hard Questions (10-40% success)**: Complex problem-solving, novel applications

### 4. Single Focus
- Test one concept or skill per question
- Avoid compound questions that test multiple unrelated concepts
- Ensure all parts of a question relate to the same learning objective

**Poor Example**: "Define photosynthesis and explain the causes of World War I"
**Good Example**: "Define photosynthesis and explain its two main stages"

### 5. Discrimination Power
- Good questions differentiate between high and low performers
- Avoid questions that everyone gets right or wrong
- Include plausible distractors in multiple-choice questions
- Test understanding, not just memorization

### 6. Fairness and Accessibility
- Avoid cultural bias or assumptions
- Use inclusive language and examples
- Consider diverse backgrounds and experiences
- Ensure questions are accessible to students with disabilities

### 7. Authenticity and Relevance
- Connect to real-world applications when possible
- Use meaningful contexts and scenarios
- Avoid artificial or contrived situations
- Make questions relevant to student interests and goals

## Question Types and Best Practices

### Multiple Choice Questions
- Use 4-5 options to reduce guessing
- Make all distractors plausible
- Avoid "all of the above" and "none of the above"
- Keep options similar in length and structure
- Place options in logical order (alphabetical, numerical)

### Short Answer Questions
- Specify expected length and format
- Provide clear scoring criteria
- Use specific prompts rather than general ones
- Consider partial credit opportunities

### Essay Questions
- Provide clear rubrics and expectations
- Specify required elements and structure
- Allow adequate time for thoughtful responses
- Consider providing choice among topics

### Problem-Solving Questions
- Break complex problems into steps
- Provide necessary formulas or references
- Show partial credit opportunities
- Include realistic scenarios and data

## Common Design Errors to Avoid

### Language Issues
- Overly complex sentence structure
- Ambiguous pronouns or references
- Jargon without definition
- Double negatives

### Content Issues
- Testing trivial details
- Requiring information not covered in course
- Mixing difficulty levels inappropriately
- Including trick questions

### Format Issues
- Inconsistent formatting
- Poor use of white space
- Unclear answer formats
- Missing instructions

### Bias Issues
- Cultural assumptions
- Gender stereotypes
- Socioeconomic assumptions
- Regional or national bias

## Quality Assurance Checklist

Before finalizing questions, check:
- [ ] Clear and unambiguous language
- [ ] Alignment with learning objectives
- [ ] Appropriate difficulty level
- [ ] Single, focused concept
- [ ] Fair and unbiased content
- [ ] Proper grammar and spelling
- [ ] Consistent formatting
- [ ] Adequate time allocation
- [ ] Clear scoring criteria
- [ ] Pilot testing completed
