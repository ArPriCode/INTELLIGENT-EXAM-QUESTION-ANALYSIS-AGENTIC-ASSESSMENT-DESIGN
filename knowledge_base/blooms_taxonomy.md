# Bloom's Taxonomy for Assessment Design

## Overview
Bloom's Taxonomy provides a hierarchical framework for categorizing educational goals and learning objectives. It helps educators design questions that target specific cognitive levels.

## Cognitive Levels (Revised Taxonomy)

### 1. Remember (Knowledge)
- **Definition**: Recall facts, basic concepts, and answers
- **Question Types**: Who, what, when, where, which, choose, find, match, list
- **Example**: "What is the capital of France?"
- **Success Rate**: 80-95%

### 2. Understand (Comprehension)  
- **Definition**: Explain ideas or concepts
- **Question Types**: Describe, explain, paraphrase, rewrite, give examples
- **Example**: "Explain why photosynthesis is important for life on Earth"
- **Success Rate**: 70-85%

### 3. Apply (Application)
- **Definition**: Use information in new situations
- **Question Types**: Apply, demonstrate, calculate, complete, illustrate, show, solve
- **Example**: "Calculate the area of a triangle with base 10cm and height 8cm"
- **Success Rate**: 60-75%

### 4. Analyze (Analysis)
- **Definition**: Draw connections among ideas
- **Question Types**: Analyze, break down, compare, contrast, diagram, deconstruct
- **Example**: "Compare and contrast the causes of World War I and World War II"
- **Success Rate**: 40-60%

### 5. Evaluate (Evaluation)
- **Definition**: Justify a stand or decision
- **Question Types**: Appraise, argue, defend, judge, select, support, value, critique
- **Example**: "Evaluate the effectiveness of renewable energy policies"
- **Success Rate**: 30-50%

### 6. Create (Synthesis)
- **Definition**: Produce new or original work
- **Question Types**: Design, formulate, build, invent, create, compose, generate, derive
- **Example**: "Design an experiment to test the effect of pH on plant growth"
- **Success Rate**: 20-40%

## Assessment Design Guidelines

### Alignment with Learning Objectives
- Match question cognitive level with intended learning outcome
- Ensure progression from lower to higher-order thinking
- Balance different cognitive levels based on course goals

### Difficulty Progression
- **Introductory courses**: Focus on Remember, Understand, Apply (70-80%)
- **Intermediate courses**: Balance Apply, Analyze, Evaluate (60-70%)
- **Advanced courses**: Emphasize Analyze, Evaluate, Create (50-60%)

### Question Design Tips
1. Use specific action verbs for each level
2. Provide clear context and instructions
3. Avoid ambiguous language
4. Consider prerequisite knowledge
5. Test one cognitive level per question

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Using high-level verbs with low-level tasks
- Mixing multiple cognitive levels in one question
- Assuming students can skip cognitive levels
- Not providing adequate scaffolding for higher levels
